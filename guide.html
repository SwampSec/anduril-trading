// how to use this guide — end-to-end workflow

This guide is not meant to be read front-to-back. Treat it as a research pipeline: each tab answers a different question. The workflow below is how hedge fund analysts layer qualitative thesis, numbers, downside risk, timing, and position sizing before putting capital at risk.
1

Understand the business

Before any ratio: what does the company sell, who pays, and why would they keep paying? Document moat, threats, and bull/bear cases.

2

Check size & valuation context

Market cap tells you liquidity, risk profile, and which multiples matter. Then run P/E, PEG, EV/EBITDA, FCF yield against peers.

3

Compare to peers

A "cheap" P/E means nothing in isolation. Score the company vs 2–4 competitors on growth, margins, returns, and leverage.

4

Stress the downside

Model a bear case: revenue drop, margin compression, debt pressure. Does equity still have value? Can the company survive?

5

Time the entry

Right thesis, wrong chart = poor returns. Read trend, support/resistance, volume, and risk patterns before you buy.

6

Size the position

Define stop-loss, risk per trade (1–2% of capital), and R/R. Use Kelly as a ceiling — most pros use half-Kelly or less.

7

Track & review

Log the trade thesis, monitor portfolio concentration and beta, paper-trade first if unsure. Revisit after earnings or narrative shifts.

8

Options — IV, Greeks & strategies

Before buying calls/puts or spreads: check implied volatility, how fast the option decays (theta), and whether your edge is direction or volatility. Use the live chain when the dashboard is running.

9

Quick reference

When a number looks odd, check the cheat sheet bands. Use acronyms search when you hit unfamiliar terms.

// reading it all holistically — the synthesis check

The 5-question pass before you buy

Run through these in order. If any answer is weak, reduce size or wait.

  1. Business: Can I explain the model in two sentences? Is the moat real or narrative?
  2. Valuation: On P/E, PEG, and EV/EBITDA vs peers — am I paying a premium? Is growth justifying it?
  3. Balance sheet: Can the company survive a 20–30% revenue shock without a dilutive raise?
  4. Technical: Is the chart confirming or fighting my thesis? Am I buying into a blow-off or breakdown?
  5. Portfolio: Does this position keep me under 1–2% risk per trade and avoid sector over-concentration?

A strong idea scores well on at least 4 of 5. A weak idea can still be traded small with a tight stop — but know which question failed.

// market cap & company size — what it is and what to look for

What is market cap?

Market capitalization is the total value the market assigns to a company's equity — not its revenue, not its debt, and not how "big" it feels from brand recognition.

Market Cap = Share Price × Shares Outstanding

Example: $150/share × 80M shares = $12B market cap. If the stock doubles but share count is unchanged, market cap doubles too. If the company issues new shares (dilution), market cap can rise while your ownership % falls.

TierTypical range (US)What to look forRisk / liquidity notes
Mega-cap > $200B Dominant franchises, index-heavy, slower % growth Highest liquidity — P/E and FCF yield usually matter more than revenue multiples
Large-cap $10B – $200B Established leaders, institutional ownership, earnings quality Deep liquidity — compare EV/EBITDA and ROIC vs sector peers
Mid-cap $2B – $10B Growth inflection, market share gains, management execution Moderate liquidity — watch earnings volatility and gap risk on news
Small-cap $300M – $2B Niche leaders, TAM expansion, balance sheet runway Thinner liquidity — position size down; slippage matters on exit

🏷️
Market cap vs enterprise value
Market cap is equity only. Enterprise value (EV) adds net debt — what an acquirer effectively pays. Use market cap for per-share math; use EV for comparing companies with different leverage.
EV = Market Cap + Total Debt − Cash
🔍
Five things to check
① Float vs market cap — low float + small cap = violent moves.
② Dilution — authorized vs outstanding shares; recent S-1/S-3 filings.
③ Index membership — S&P 500 inclusion forces passive buying.
④ Which multiple fits — mega-caps: P/E + FCF; pre-profit: P/S; leveraged: EV/EBITDA.
⑤ Cap vs TAM — $2B cap in a $200B TAM ≠ $2B cap in a $3B niche.
⚠️
Common mistakes
Confusing cheap stock price with cheap valuation ($5 stock with 500M shares can be expensive). Ignoring dilution from stock comp and convertibles. Comparing P/E across different cap tiers without adjusting for growth and risk.

// dcf valuation — how to price a business

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

A DCF values a business as the sum of all future cash flows discounted back to today. If your DCF gives a value higher than the current stock price, the stock may be undervalued.

Intrinsic Value = Σ [ FCFₜ / (1 + WACC)ᵗ ] + Terminal Value

The biggest variable is your growth rate assumption and WACC (weighted average cost of capital). Hedge funds stress-test multiple scenarios: bear, base, bull.

// reading price action and charts

Technical analysis reads price action and volume to time entries and exits. Hedge funds use it alongside fundamentals — you might have the right thesis but terrible timing if you ignore the chart.
// candlestick anatomy
The Body

The thick rectangle. It spans from the opening price to the closing price. A green body means price closed higher than it opened — buyers won the session. A red body means price closed lower — sellers won. A wide body = strong conviction. A tiny body = indecision (see Doji).

The Wicks (Shadows)

The thin lines extending above and below the body. The upper wick shows the highest price reached during the session — buyers tried to push higher but failed to hold it. The lower wick shows the lowest price — sellers tried to push lower but got rejected. Long wicks = lots of intraday fighting.

O Open — where the session started

The price when the market opened (or the first trade of the period). On a bullish candle it's the bottom of the body — price started here and rose. On a bearish candle it's the top of the body — price started here and fell. Driven by overnight news, gaps, pre-market orders.

C Close — where the session ended

The final traded price of the period. The most important price — it determines colour and reflects the net verdict of all buyers and sellers for that session. On a bullish candle, close is the top of the body. Driven by institutional positioning into the close, earnings, macro data releases.

H High — the session's price ceiling

The highest price traded during the period — the tip of the upper wick. It marks where sellers overpowered buyers. A high equal to the close (no upper wick) means buyers were in control all the way to the end — very bullish. A long upper wick means sellers slapped price back hard from that high.

L Low — the session's price floor

The lowest price traded — tip of the lower wick. It marks where buyers stepped in and absorbed selling. A low equal to the open (no lower wick on a bearish candle) means sellers held control throughout. A long lower wick means buyers rescued price and drove it back up — a key reversal signal at support.

// what drives each component
Open is driven by:
Overnight news, earnings releases after close, gap-ups from catalyst, pre-market futures positioning, institutional orders sitting at the open.
Close is driven by:
End-of-day institutional rebalancing, index fund buying, options expiry pinning, macro data (CPI, jobs), last-minute momentum from retail traders.
High/Low (wicks) driven by:
Stop-loss hunts (algos push to trigger stops then reverse), liquidity grabs, intraday news spikes, thin order books in early/late trading hours.
Body size driven by:
Conviction — big body = one side dominated all session. Small body = back-and-forth, no clear winner. Volume confirms: big body on low volume = weak signal.

// key indicators

RSI — Relative Strength Index

Measures momentum on a 0–100 scale. RSI above 70 means the asset may be overbought (stretched to the upside, potential pullback). RSI below 30 means oversold (potential bounce). Divergence — price makes a new high but RSI doesn't — is a powerful reversal warning.

RSI > 70 — Overbought RSI 30–70 — Neutral RSI < 30 — Oversold

MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence

Plots the difference between a 12-period and 26-period EMA, with a 9-period signal line. When MACD crosses above the signal line: bullish. When it crosses below: bearish. The histogram shows the gap between MACD and signal — widening means strengthening momentum.

MACD = EMA(12) − EMA(26) | Signal = EMA(9) of MACD

Bollinger Bands

Three lines: a 20-period MA in the middle, and upper/lower bands 2 standard deviations away. Price touching the upper band in an uptrend = strength. Price walking the lower band = weakness. A squeeze (bands narrowing) signals a big move coming — direction is then determined by the breakout.

// quantitative risk & return concepts

Quant tools turn gut feelings into measurable metrics. Even discretionary (non-quant) funds use these to manage risk and size positions correctly. If you can't measure it, you can't manage it.
ConceptWhat it measuresSignal
Beta (β) Sensitivity to the market. β=1.5 → moves 1.5× the index. β<1 → defensive. Risk exposure
Alpha (α) Excess return above what beta would predict. Pure skill, not market riding. Skill metric
Sharpe Ratio (Return − Risk-free rate) ÷ Std deviation. >1 good, >2 excellent. Risk-adj return
Sortino Ratio Like Sharpe but only penalizes downside volatility. Better for asymmetric strategies. Downside risk
Max Drawdown Largest peak-to-trough loss. A fund down 50% needs a 100% gain to recover. Survival metric
Correlation −1 to +1. Low or negative correlation between positions = real diversification. Diversification
Z-Score Std deviations from the mean. Used in pair trading to find stretched relationships. Mean reversion
Value at Risk (VaR) Maximum expected loss at a confidence level (e.g., 95% confidence you won't lose >X% in a day). Tail risk
Factor Exposure Sensitivity to Value, Momentum, Quality, Low Volatility, Size factors (Fama-French). Return attribution
Information Ratio Alpha ÷ Tracking Error. How consistently you beat the benchmark. Consistency

// position sizing — the kelly criterion

Kelly Criterion

Tells you what fraction of your portfolio to bet, given your edge. Most funds use a "half-Kelly" to reduce variance.

f* = (bp − q) / b | b=odds, p=win probability, q=1−p

Example: 60% win rate, 2:1 payoff → f* = (2×0.6 − 0.4) / 2 = 40% of portfolio (use 20% with half-Kelly).

// backtesting workflow

1. Define hypothesis
Articulate the thesis before looking at data. "Stocks with RSI < 30 and positive FCF outperform over 6 months."
2. Gather clean data
Use yfinance, Quandl, or OpenBB. Adjust for splits and dividends. Clean missing values.
3. Code the strategy
Use Backtrader or Zipline. Define entry/exit rules as code — not discretionary judgment.
4. Out-of-sample test
Keep 30% of data unseen during development. Test on it at the end. In-sample results alone are meaningless.
5. Analyze results
Sharpe ratio, max drawdown, win rate, profit factor. Use QuantStats for a full tearsheet.
6. Avoid overfitting
Fewer parameters = more robust. If you need 12 conditions to make it work, it probably won't live.

// the 6-step annotation workflow

01

Mark support & resistance

Find price levels where the chart has bounced or been rejected multiple times. Draw horizontal lines. The more touches, the stronger the level. These become your battlegrounds.

02

Draw the trendline

Connect two or more higher lows (uptrend) or lower highs (downtrend) with a straight line. A break of the trendline is often the first warning of a trend change.

03

Add moving averages

Drop MA20 (short-term trend) and MA50 (medium-term trend) onto the chart. Price above both MAs = bullish structure. The golden cross (MA20 crosses above MA50) is a buy signal.

04

Apply Bollinger Bands

Watch for BB squeezes (bands narrowing) — they precede explosive moves. When price breaks out of a squeeze on high volume, that's the direction signal.

05

Check RSI at key levels

When price reaches a support level AND RSI is below 35, that's a high-probability long setup. When price hits resistance AND RSI is above 65, consider reducing exposure.

06

Mark entries, stops, targets

// how to place your stop-loss and profit target

Risk/Reward Ratio

Never enter a trade without defining your stop and target first. Aim for at least 2:1 reward-to-risk — meaning for every $1 you risk, you should stand to make $2.

R/R = (Target − Entry) ÷ (Entry − Stop-Loss) | Minimum: 2.0

Example: Entry $100, Stop $96 (risk $4), Target $110 (reward $10) → R/R = 2.5. At 50% win rate, this strategy is profitable over time.

// core equations to run on every earnings report

EPS Surprise %

How much did the company beat or miss analyst expectations? A positive surprise drives the stock up; a miss drives it down. Anything beyond ±5% is significant.

EPS Surprise % = ((Reported EPS − Estimated EPS) ÷ |Estimated EPS|) × 100

Example: Reported $1.25 EPS vs $1.10 estimate → Surprise = +13.6% → bullish catalyst.

Revenue Growth (YoY & QoQ)

Year-over-year tells you how the business is growing in absolute terms. Quarter-over-quarter shows acceleration or deceleration — hedge funds care about the second derivative (is growth speeding up or slowing down?).

YoY Growth % = ((This Quarter Revenue − Same Quarter Last Year) ÷ Same Quarter Last Year) × 100
QoQ Growth % = ((This Quarter Revenue − Last Quarter Revenue) ÷ Last Quarter Revenue) × 100

Gross Margin & Operating Margin

Expanding margins = pricing power or cost efficiency improving. Contracting margins = competition is pricing them lower or costs are rising faster than revenue. These are the most important trend lines in a growth company.

Gross Margin % = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue × 100
Operating Margin % = EBIT ÷ Revenue × 100
Net Margin % = Net Income ÷ Revenue × 100

Earnings Yield & Forward P/E

After earnings, re-run your valuation. Use the new annualized EPS to see what you're actually paying. Forward P/E uses next twelve months (NTM) estimated earnings — this is what the market is pricing.

Earnings Yield = EPS (TTM) ÷ Stock Price × 100   (inverse of P/E)
Forward P/E = Current Stock Price ÷ NTM Estimated EPS
PEG Ratio = Forward P/E ÷ Expected EPS Growth Rate   (<1 = potentially undervalued)

Free Cash Flow Conversion

High FCF conversion means earnings are real cash, not accounting artifacts. A ratio consistently below 70% deserves scrutiny — where is the cash going?

FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures
FCF Conversion % = FCF ÷ Net Income × 100   (target: >80%)
FCF Yield = FCF per Share ÷ Stock Price × 100   (>5% = attractive)

Operating Leverage

If revenue grows 10% and operating income grows 25%, that's positive operating leverage — fixed costs are being spread over more revenue. This is the holy grail in growth investing.

Operating Leverage = % Change in EBIT ÷ % Change in Revenue

Ratio >1 means margins are expanding as revenue grows. Ratio <1 means costs are growing faster than revenue — a red flag.

Risk isn't just about losing money — it's about recognising the warning signs before the damage is done. Every major crash, squeeze, and blow-off top leaves fingerprints on the chart. Learn to read them before you're inside one.

// risk scenario simulation

What a blow-off top looks like

A normal uptrend has rhythmic higher-highs and higher-lows with healthy pullbacks. A blow-off top is when that trend goes parabolic — price doubles or triples in a short window, volume explodes 3-5× average, and RSI stays pinned above 80. When it reverses, it reverses violently — often giving back 50–80% of the entire parabolic move in a fraction of the time it took to go up.

Warning signal: 3+ consecutive large-body bullish candles + volume >3× 20-day average + RSI >80

// visual risk signal dictionary

⚠ Extreme danger

Blow-off top

Slow grind, then near-vertical price action, then violent reversal. The top forms on maximum euphoria and maximum volume.

⚠ Short squeeze trap

Short squeeze

Heavy short interest + catalyst = shorts forced to buy to cover, amplifying the move. The squeeze exhausts itself fast — do not chase.

⚠ Liquidity crisis

Flash crash

Vertical drop in seconds to minutes — no buyers, stop-losses cascade. Often recovers just as fast. Caused by algo feedback loops or liquidity gaps.

⚠ Distribution

Distribution top

Institutions quietly sell into retail buyers at the top. Sideways chop with declining volume while price barely moves — smart money is exiting.

⚠ Breakdown

Support failure

Support holds multiple times then breaks. Key warning: a failed retest of the broken level (old support becomes new resistance) confirms the breakdown.

⚠ Hidden risk

Bearish divergence

Price makes new highs but RSI makes lower highs. Momentum is fading while price still rises — a warning that the move is losing fuel.

⚠ Capitulation

Panic selling

Exhaustion selling — the last weak holders throw in the towel. Identified by a giant red candle with huge volume. Often marks a short-term or long-term bottom.

⚠ Trap

Bull trap

Price breaks above resistance — buyers pile in — then immediately reverses back through. Traders who bought the breakout are now underwater.

// the 5 rules for surviving violent moves

🛑
Pre-set your stop
Before entering any trade, define the exact price where you exit if wrong. A stop-loss placed after you're already down is a hope, not a plan.
📏
Size for the stop, not the dream
Position size = (Account × Max risk %) ÷ (Entry − Stop). Never risk more than 1–2% of total capital on a single trade.
Size = (Capital × 0.01) ÷ (Entry − Stop)
🚫
Never chase a parabola
If you missed the move, you missed it. Buying something because it's already up 80% is buying other people's exit liquidity. Wait for the base to form.
💧
Respect illiquidity
Thin markets (low float stocks, small-cap crypto) move 10× faster in both directions. What goes up 40% in a day can go down 60% the next. Size positions accordingly.
📉
Correlation spikes in crashes
When markets crash, almost everything goes down together — your "diversified" portfolio suddenly moves as one. Keep cash or inverse positions as a true hedge.

// how to value crypto assets

NVT Ratio — Network Value to Transactions

The crypto equivalent of a P/E ratio. Compares the total market cap to the dollar value of transactions flowing through the network. High NVT = network is overvalued relative to usage.

NVT = Market Cap ÷ Daily On-chain Transaction Volume (30-day MA)

NVT > 90 historically signals overvaluation. NVT < 25 signals undervaluation relative to network activity.

Stock-to-Flow (S2F) — Scarcity Model

Originally a commodity valuation model (gold, silver), applied to Bitcoin. Measures scarcity: how many years of current production does the existing supply represent? Higher = scarcer = higher predicted value. Bitcoin's S2F doubles every halving.

Stock-to-Flow = Current Supply ÷ Annual New Supply (production rate)

Bitcoin post-2024 halving: ~50 years S2F — comparable to gold (~62). Critics note S2F breaks down when demand isn't anchored to scarcity alone.

Market Cap vs Realised Cap (MVRV)

Realised Cap values each coin at the price it last moved on-chain — a proxy for the average cost basis of all holders. MVRV compares current market cap to this. High MVRV = most holders are in profit (potential sell pressure). Low MVRV = most holders underwater (potential bottom).

MVRV = Market Cap ÷ Realised Cap   (MVRV > 3.5 = historically overheated)

// what is an option?

CALL option

Gives you the right to BUY 100 shares at the strike price before expiry. You buy a call when you expect the stock to rise. Max loss = premium paid. Max gain = unlimited.

Profit = (Stock Price − Strike) × 100 − Premium Paid
BullishRight to buyMax loss = premium

PUT option

Gives you the right to SELL 100 shares at the strike price before expiry. You buy a put when you expect the stock to fall. Max loss = premium paid. Max gain = strike price (stock → $0).

Profit = (Strike − Stock Price) × 100 − Premium Paid
BearishRight to sellMax loss = premium

// the greeks — measuring option sensitivity

Delta (Δ)

How much the option price moves per $1 move in the stock. Ranges 0–1 for calls, 0 to −1 for puts. Also roughly the probability the option expires ITM.

Delta 0.5 → option moves $0.50 per $1 stock move
Deep ITM ≈ 1.0  |  ATM ≈ 0.5  |  OTM ≈ 0.1–0.3

Gamma (Γ)

Rate of change of delta per $1 move. Highest at ATM near expiry. High gamma = delta changes fast — cuts both ways. Dangerous when short options near expiry.

New Delta = Old Delta + Gamma × Price Change
Spikes dramatically in final week before expiry

Theta (Θ)

Daily time decay — how much value the option loses each day just from time passing. Always negative for option buyers, positive for sellers. Accelerates as expiry approaches.

Option value tomorrow ≈ Today's value + Theta
Theta −$0.05/day = option loses $5/day per contract

Vega (ν)

Sensitivity to implied volatility. 1% rise in IV → option gains Vega dollars. Buy options before IV spikes (earnings). Sell options when IV is high and expected to drop.

P&L from IV change = Vega × ΔIV%
Vega 0.20 + IV rises 5% → +$1.00 per share = +$100/contract

Rho (ρ)

Sensitivity to interest rate changes. Usually small for short-dated options. Calls have positive rho (benefit from rate rises), puts have negative rho. Important for LEAPS.

Rho 0.05 → 1% rate rise adds $0.05 to call value
Mostly irrelevant for short-dated options (<90 DTE)

IV Crush

When implied volatility collapses after a catalyst (earnings, FDA decision), option premiums crater even if the stock moves your direction. The #1 options trap for beginners.

IV before earnings: 80% → After: 30% → Options lose ~40–60% of value
Avoid buying options just before earnings unless the move will be massive

// market events, halts, and circuit breakers explained

The market has safety mechanisms, scheduled events, and shock events that can move prices violently. Knowing what each one is — and what it means for your position — is non-negotiable.

// trading halts — what they are and why they happen

Regulatory halt (T1/T2)

The SEC or exchange halts trading in a specific stock pending news dissemination. Lasts minutes to hours. You cannot buy or sell during a halt. When it reopens, the price can gap dramatically — up or down — based on whatever news caused the halt.

⚠ Common before M&A announcements, FDA decisions, fraud investigations

Volatility halt (LULD)

Limit Up-Limit Down. If a stock moves more than a set % in 5 minutes, trading pauses for 5 minutes. Prevents flash crashes from spreading. Thresholds: ±5% for S&P 500 stocks, ±10% for others, ±20% for stocks under $3.

Automatic — triggered by price movement speed

IPO/spin-off opening delay

New listings don't open at 9:30am. Market makers must match buy/sell orders first. Can take hours. The opening print is often a volatile gap from the reference price as the real market-clearing price is discovered.

Reference price ≠ where it will open

Market-wide circuit breakers

If the S&P 500 drops a set amount from the prior close, all US equity trading halts. Resets daily. Level 3 = market closes for the day.

L1: −7% → 15 min halt  L2: −13% → 15 min halt  L3: −20% → Market closes

// understanding gaps — the 4 types

Common gap

Small gap in normal trading. Usually fills within days. No major catalyst.

Breakaway gap

Breaks out of a consolidation on high volume. Signals start of a new trend. Often doesn't fill for weeks/months.

Runaway gap

Occurs mid-trend on strong momentum — trend accelerating. Signals strong conviction. Rarely fills until trend ends.

Exhaustion gap

Final gap at end of a trend on exhaustion. Quickly fills as price reverses. One of the most reliable reversal signals.

// dividends & funds — income investing explained

Not all investing is about buying growth stocks and hoping for price appreciation. Dividends and funds are how most of the world's wealth is quietly compounded — understanding them is non-negotiable whether you're building income, diversifying, or just evaluating a company that pays one.

// part 1 — what is a dividend?

The basic concept

A dividend is a portion of a company's profits paid directly to shareholders — usually quarterly, sometimes monthly or annually. It's the company saying: "We made more money than we need to reinvest, so here's your share." You receive cash per share you own, deposited directly into your brokerage account.

Annual dividend income = DPS × Shares owned
Dividend yield = Annual DPS ÷ Stock price × 100

Why companies pay dividends

Mature companies with stable cash flows and limited high-return reinvestment opportunities return cash to shareholders. Dividend payers tend to be older, established businesses — utilities, consumer staples, financials, REITs. Growth companies (MRVL, NVDA at early stages) reinvest everything instead. Paying a dividend signals financial maturity and discipline.

Stable cash flowsMature businessShareholder return

The power of reinvestment (DRIP)

A Dividend Reinvestment Plan (DRIP) automatically uses your dividends to buy more shares instead of paying cash. This creates compounding — your share count grows every quarter, which increases next quarter's dividend, which buys more shares. Einstein reportedly called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world.

Future value = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t)
P=principal, r=annual return, n=periods/year, t=years

// the four critical dividend dates

The ex-dividend date is the one that matters most. If you buy shares on or after the ex-dividend date, you do NOT receive the upcoming dividend — the previous owner does. The stock price also typically drops by approximately the dividend amount on the ex-date, because the cash value has left the company's books. Buying just before ex-date to "capture" the dividend doesn't work — you pay for it in the price drop.

// dividend traps — when high yield is a warning, not a gift

⚠ Danger

Yield trap

A yield of 12%+ almost always means the market expects the dividend to be cut. The stock price has already fallen, mechanically inflating the yield. If the cut happens, you lose both the income and capital.

Check: Is payout ratio >80%? Is FCF covering the dividend?
⚠ Danger

Dividend cut

When a company reduces or eliminates its dividend, the stock typically falls 20–40% instantly as income investors sell en masse. The cut itself matters less than what it signals about the underlying business.

Red flags: rising payout ratio, falling FCF, heavy debt load
⚠ Caution

Borrowing to pay dividends

Some companies take on debt to maintain or grow dividends when earnings are insufficient. This is unsustainable and destroys long-term value. Check if net debt is rising while earnings are flat.

FCF payout >100% = company is funding dividends with debt or asset sales
⚠ Caution

Ex-date buying trap

Buying just before the ex-dividend date to "capture" the dividend doesn't generate free money. The stock drops by ~the dividend amount on ex-date. You simply convert price to income and pay taxes on it.

Stock at $50 pays $1 dividend → opens ex-date at ~$49

// fund metrics — what to look at before buying

Expense ratio

The annual fee charged as a % of your investment, deducted automatically. It compounds against you over time. On $100,000 over 30 years, a 1% expense ratio costs you ~$174,000 vs 0.05% costing ~$16,000.

Annual cost = Portfolio value × Expense ratio
Rule: never pay more than 0.5% for a passive fund. Active must prove it earns its fee.

NAV (Net Asset Value)

The per-share value of a fund's assets minus liabilities. For mutual funds, it's calculated once at end of day. For ETFs, the market price trades around NAV — a premium or discount can exist temporarily.

NAV = (Total assets − Liabilities) ÷ Shares outstanding
Premium/discount to NAV: (Market price − NAV) ÷ NAV × 100

Tracking error

For index funds and ETFs — how closely does the fund actually follow its benchmark? A low tracking error means the fund is doing its job. High tracking error on a passive fund is a red flag.

Tracking error = Std deviation of (Fund return − Index return)
<0.10% = excellent for passive  |  >0.50% = investigate why

Distribution yield vs SEC yield

Distribution yield = what the fund paid out over the last 12 months ÷ current price. SEC yield (30-day) = standardised forward-looking yield. For bond funds, always look at SEC yield — it's more accurate for what you'll actually receive.

Distribution yield = Annual distributions ÷ Current NAV × 100

Alpha & Sharpe (for active funds)

Alpha = excess return above the benchmark after adjusting for risk. A fund with positive alpha over 10+ years is genuinely skilled. Sharpe ratio shows risk-adjusted return. Most active funds show negative alpha after fees over long periods.

Alpha = Fund return − [Risk-free rate + Beta × (Market return − Risk-free rate)]

Turnover rate

How often the fund replaces its holdings in a year. High turnover (100%+) means the manager is trading constantly — generating transaction costs and, in taxable accounts, capital gains distributions that you pay tax on even if you didn't sell.

Index funds: 2–10% turnover  |  Active: 50–200%+ common

// penny stocks — high risk, low price, brutal reality

⚠ Critical warning — read before anything else

The SEC estimates that the majority of penny stock promotions are fraudulent. These are not undervalued gems — they are overwhelmingly failing companies, shell companies, or active fraud vehicles. The same features that make them exciting (huge % moves, low entry price) are the exact features that make them lethal to retail investors.

❌ No audited financials required on OTC
❌ Spreads can be 10–50% wide
❌ Market makers control price freely
❌ Pump-and-dump is rampant and legal to coordinate in grey areas
❌ Liquidity can vanish instantly — you may not be able to sell
❌ Many companies have no revenue, product, or viable business
With those warnings clearly stated: penny stocks are a real market, they are legally traded, and understanding how they work protects you whether you choose to participate or not. Some legitimate small companies do trade here early in their life cycle. The goal of this section is to give you the full picture — what they are, where they trade, how manipulation works, and how the very small number of traders who profit consistently do it.

// what exactly is a penny stock?

The SEC definition

The SEC defines a penny stock as any equity security trading below $5.00 per share that is not listed on a national exchange and does not meet certain net asset or revenue thresholds. This is broader than most people think — a $4.80 stock on a major exchange is NOT a penny stock by the legal definition. True penny stocks are OTC (over-the-counter) market securities.

SEC Rule 15g-9 governs broker-dealer requirements for penny stock transactions

Colloquial vs legal

In common use, "penny stock" usually means stocks trading below $1 — sometimes below $0.10 (sub-penny). These are almost exclusively OTC market securities. At sub-penny prices, a move from $0.001 to $0.002 is a 100% gain — this is why they attract speculative attention. But that same move can happen in either direction in minutes.

<$5 SEC definition <$1 common usage <$0.01 sub-penny

Why companies end up here

Companies trade as penny stocks for a few reasons: they're early-stage startups that couldn't list on a major exchange, they were delisted from NYSE/Nasdaq for failing standards, they're shell companies seeking a reverse merger with a private company, or they were created specifically as a fraud vehicle. Understanding which category a stock falls into is the most important first question.

// how OTC trading actually works — the mechanics

Market makers control the spread

Unlike exchange stocks where an order book matches buyers and sellers, OTC stocks are traded through market makers who quote bid and ask prices and profit from the spread. In penny stocks this spread can be 10–50% or more. If you buy at the ask and immediately sell at the bid, you could lose 20–30% instantly before the stock moves at all.

Effective cost = (Ask − Bid) ÷ Ask × 100%
Example: Bid $0.008, Ask $0.012 → 33% spread. You need a 50% gain just to break even.

Level 2 quotes

Level 2 shows the full order book — every market maker's bid and ask, the size of each order, and who is quoting. In penny stocks, Level 2 reveals who is accumulating, who is distributing, and how thin the market is. Serious penny stock traders watch Level 2 constantly. A single market maker controlling both sides of the book is a major manipulation warning.

Key: NITE, CDEL, ETRF, VIRT are common OTC market makers

Volume and float

Float = shares available for public trading (total shares minus insider/restricted shares). Low float stocks (under 10M shares) are easiest to move — small buying pressure creates large price swings. Penny stock traders look for low float + high volume as a catalyst setup. But the same dynamics work against you when you're trying to exit.

Days to trade out = Position size ÷ (Avg daily volume × % you can realistically move)

No short selling (mostly)

Most penny stocks cannot be short sold because brokers can't borrow the shares. This removes a natural price correction mechanism — there's no force pushing prices back down when they're inflated by promotion. It's one reason pumps can run longer than logic suggests before collapsing. Some specialised brokers (Interactive Brokers) do borrow penny stock shares at extremely high cost.

// if you choose to trade penny stocks — the rules that matter

The traders who consistently make money in penny stocks are not gambling on promotions — they are momentum traders with strict rules, fast execution, and an immediate exit plan. They buy early in a move, take profits quickly, and never hold overnight. They treat each trade as a hot potato, not an investment.
⏱️
Time horizon = hours, not days
Penny stock promotions peak and collapse within 1–3 days, often within hours of the main promotion blast. If you're holding a penny stock overnight you are speculating, not trading. Exit before the close or be prepared to open to a 50% gap down.
📏
Position size = maximum 1% of account
Given that penny stocks can go to zero, your position size must reflect the realistic worst case. A 1% position going to zero costs you 1%. A 10% position going to zero is catastrophic. Serious traders often use 0.25–0.5% per penny stock trade.
🚪
Know your exit before your entry
The most important rule in all trading applies double here. Before you buy: at what price will you sell for a profit? At what price will you accept you're wrong and cut? In illiquid markets, you must use limit orders — market orders in penny stocks can fill at catastrophic prices.
🚫
Never average down
In penny stocks, a falling price almost never means "getting cheaper" — it means someone with a larger position is selling. If you buy at $0.05 and it falls to $0.03, adding more does not lower your average meaningfully and doubles your exposure to a stock in a distribution phase.

Liquidity check before size
If you want to buy $1,000 of a stock doing $5,000 in daily dollar volume, you are 20% of the market. Your own buying will move the price against you on entry and your selling will crater it on exit. Never take a position you can't exit in under 10% of daily volume.

Real catalyst vs paid promotion
The only legitimate reason to be in a penny stock is a real, verifiable catalyst: FDA approval, major contract announcement, reverse merger with a credible private company, or genuine turnaround. A stock promotion email is not a catalyst — it is the catalyst for you to avoid it.
